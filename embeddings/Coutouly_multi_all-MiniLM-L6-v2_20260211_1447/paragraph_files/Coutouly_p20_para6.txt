Finally, the presence of pressure- flaked microblade technology has also been found at the 
Nogahabara site in the interior of northwestern 
Alaska. Odess and Rasic (2007) proposed this 
assemblage represented a single Late Pleistocene occupation; however, Holmes et al. (2008) 
suggest the site is more likely a palimpsest or 
mixed assemblage. Given the surface context of 
the site, the chronology is not well established. 
The assemblage is made up of microcores, microblades, burins, lanceolate projectile points, and 
side-notched projectile points. The microblade 
component includes a microblade core, microblade-core preforms, core tablets, and microblades (Odess and Rasic 2007), all of it obsidian. 
The microblade core recovered at Nogahabara 
(Fig. 16), the tallest pressure- flaked microblade 
core I have seen in Alaska so far, also seems to 
correspond to a Mode 3 type of pressure flaking 
given the size of the core, its microblade removals, and the associated microblades (Odess and 
Rasic 2007:Fig. 8).
