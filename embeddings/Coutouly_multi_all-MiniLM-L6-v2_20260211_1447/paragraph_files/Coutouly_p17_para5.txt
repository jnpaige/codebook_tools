During the Late Pleistocene and the Early Holocene, microblade technology is widespread 
in central Alaska at sites assigned to the Denali 
complex or related complexes, such as Swan 
Point, Dry Creek, Panguingue Creek, and Donnelly 
Ridge (Goebel and Bigelow 1992; Gómez Coutouly 
2011b, 2012; Hoffecker et al. 1996; Holmes 2011; 
West 1967). These assemblages, as well as other 
Denali- related assemblages from central Alaska, 
are dominated by Campus- style microblade cores 
(wedge-shaped microblade cores), although there 
is at least one occupation (Swan Point CZ4b) that 
has Yubetsu- style microblade cores (Gómez Coutouly 2012; Holmes 2011). The Campus cores are 
technologically different from the Amakomanak 
collection. At least one site from central Alaska, 
Panguingue Creek, has microblade cores of similar 
form. This site, located in the Nenana Valley, dating to ca. 7500–5850 BC (Goebel and Bigelow 1992; 
Gómez Coutouly 2011b), has a microblade technology based on prismatic cores (Fig. 13) that are 
typologically close to the ones from Amakomanak.
