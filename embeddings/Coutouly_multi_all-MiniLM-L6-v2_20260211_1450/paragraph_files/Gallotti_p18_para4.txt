The technique used to extract such pieces is direct hand-held
percussion. The patterns observed in the core analysis are also
observed on most of the resulting large ﬂakes. The majority of these
ﬂakes (n ¼ 21) display a natural central part of the dorsal face,
peripheral centripetal removals and wide, thick facetted butts,
which are often adjacent to one of their backed lateral edges. In
eight examples, these ﬂakes resemble large éclats débordants
(Fig. 17: 3). However, three ﬂakes exhibit invasive centripetal and
cordal negative scars on the dorsal face, which documents an
intense preparation of the convexity of the ﬂaking surface during
the débitage. Only three of these ﬂakes were retouched. One is a
short, very wide ﬂake of Melka Fault lava modiﬁed by large notches
on the dorsal face (Fig. 17: 4). The second example is a large
transversal ﬂake of porphyritic basalt with a unidirectional facetted
butt, which was turned into a massive scraper by a bifacial, rather
invasive and non-intensive retouch. The retouch created a convex
cutting edge and did not modify the shape of the blank. Two large
removals on the ventral face thin the bulb and partially remove the
butt (Fig. 17: 5). The third example is a transversal large ﬂake of
porphyritic basalt with centripetal invasive removals on the dorsal
face that correspond to the convexity conﬁguration of the core and
a fairly invasive and continuous retouch on one edge of the ventral
face, which thinned and removed the butt-bulb part. Isolated and
non-continuous small removals were produced to thin portions of
the edges (Fig. 17: 6). These last two artefacts can hardly be
considered bifaces sensu stricto because they do not exhibit the
search for a bifacial or bilateral equilibrium and a preconceived
form (Roche and Texier, 1991; Inizan et al., 1999).
