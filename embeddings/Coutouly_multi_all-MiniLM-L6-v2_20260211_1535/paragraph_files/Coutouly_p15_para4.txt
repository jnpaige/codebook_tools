Out of the 80 recorded microblades from 
Amakomanak, I selected 75 of them that could fit 
the criteria for pressure flaking. I then compared 
them to the metric data of experimental microblades (Fig. 12). The metric data presented here 
apply only to flint/chert material and are based 
on the experimental results made by Pelegrin. 
For obsidian, a much more fragile material, experimental research gives different results for the 
production of microblades and blades, not only in 
terms of size (wider and longer microblades) but 
also because there is no need for a core- holding or 
core- immobilizing device in some cases (Pelegrin 
1988, 2003, 2012). The width of the Amakomanak 
microblade cores excludes the sole use of Mode 
1 removal but does not absolutely confirm the 
presence of Mode 3, though it does suggest it. 
Although some microblades are wider than 10 mm 
(which could exclude modes 1 and 2), they are 
not the most characteristic pressure- flaked microblades (such as Fig. 9a). The metric data is, however, quite consistent with a mixture of different 
modes, which is not surprising when taking into 
account the presence of smaller microblade cores 
(as discussed above). Most microblades (fragments) 
have a length between 18 mm and 35 mm, and two 
of the longest microblade scars are about 40 mm 
and 58 mm. This data also indicates an overlap 
between modes 2 and 3.
