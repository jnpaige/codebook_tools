Pressure-Flaking Techniques
Pressure flaking was the main technique for the 
removal of microblades, based on the regularity of 
microblades and microblade scars on cores. Key 
features of pressure flaking include edge regularity and parallelism, a straight rather than curved 
profile, maximum width at the shoulder (i.e., 
immediately below the bulb), and the presence of 
a very small point-like pressure bulb (for detailed 
descriptions of pressure- flaking attributes, see 
Desrosiers 2012; Gómez Coutouly 2012; Inizan et 
al. 1999; Tixier 1984). Donald E. Crabtree’s experiments on pressure flaking and blade production 
clearly demonstrated that “the wider the blade, the 
greater the amount of pressure that is required” to 
produce it (Crabtree 1968:468). Jacques Pelegrin 
(1988, 2003, 2012) has experimented with several 
different pressure techniques (termed “modes”) 
for the removal of blades and microblades in 
order to reproduce various archaeological examples that vary widely in size from small microblades to very large blades (Gómez Coutouly 
2011a, 2011b, 2016). As a result, he proposed five 
main pressure- flaking modes for the production 
of microblades and blades: handheld (Mode 1), 
shoulder crutch (Mode 2), short crutch (Mode 3), 
long crutch (Mode 4), and pressure with a lever 
device (Mode 5). In sum, modes 1 and 2 produce 
microblades, Mode 3 produces slightly larger

- Discoid. Discoid technology was an important aspect of the
small débitage (n ¼ 171; Fig. 9). Discoid cores present the same
patterns described for obsidian specimens. When the core is
not at its maximal exploitation phase, it is possible to recognise
that the natural blanks were cobbles of lavas or cobbles and
angular elements of obsidian. Discoid cores exhibit two convex,
usually asymmetric surfaces. These surfaces are the ﬂaking
surface and the striking platform. Flakes are detached according to a plane that is secant to the plane of intersection of the
two surfaces (sub-parallel when the core is overexploited). The
striking platform could be any one of the following: (1) a natural surface with an ideal convexity for knapping, (2) a convex
surface that corresponds to the ventral or dorsal face of a ﬂake,
prepared or not, (3) a surface prepared by detachments on its
periphery worked before the ﬂaking surface was created, or (4)
two fairly similar symmetric surfaces, created by removals,
which could be employed simultaneously or in alternate series
of removals as a striking platform and ﬂaking surface (Boëda,
1993; Jaubert and Mourre, 1996; Mourre, 2003; Terradas,
2003). The convexity of the ﬂaking surface is natural in the
use of cobbles, whereas angular elements require an initial
conﬁguration of the core’s geometry. The peripheral convexity
is acquired by a mostly centripetal ﬂaking organisation and
tangential removals (Fig. 11: 5e14). The ﬂaking surface of one
obsidian core presents a ﬂake negative scar similar to that of a
preferential ﬂake (Fig. 11: 6, 13). Some ﬂakes, which were detached from the ventral face of the ﬂake blank, are Kombewa
ﬂakes (Fig. 11: 7).
