Except for a few obsidian cores showing a simple débitage,
predetermined methods were adopted to produce large ﬂakes as
potential blanks for LCTs: discoid for obsidian and unifacial centripetal prepared exploitation for porphyritic basalt and Melka
Fault lava. Although speciﬁc débitage processes were carried out,
large ﬂakes present in the assemblage do not show similar
morphological or metrical patterns, but only a great thickness and,
in most cases, a width larger than the length. In addition, large
ﬂakes rarely appear to ﬁll the role of blanks to be turned into LCTs
because they were not frequently retouched. When retouch
occurred, it was a scraper-like retouch, as only the very edges of
pieces were modiﬁed and the overall shape of the blanks did not
change. The absence of a search for speciﬁc morphological traits
does not allow the identiﬁcation of shaping in LCT production.
When removals penetrated the blank volume and were not
restricted to the edges, the objective was to thin the butt-bulb
portion and not to intervene in the volume balance. At any rate,
the term ‘shaping’ is not warranted in its original meaning, as this
speciﬁc process is not repeated on a certain number of specimens
that all tend towards the same morphology: the knapper’s objective was to modify edges, not “to manufacture one single object
with a deﬁnite morphology” (Roche, 2005:40). The absence of tool
types led us to classify these massive retouched pieces into the
general class of LCTs.
