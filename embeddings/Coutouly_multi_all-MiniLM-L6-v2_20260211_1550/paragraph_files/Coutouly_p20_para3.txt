some discussed in this paper) may be a derivative 
from the Mesolithic Sumnagin complex of Siberia 
(ca. 10700 to 5100 BC). However, the prismatic 
cores from northwestern Alaska are not that close 
morphologically or technologically from the 
Sumnagin Mesolithic cores. The Sumnagin microblade cores always tend to be rounded conical- or 
bullet- shaped cores with facetted platforms with 
microblade removals all around the circumference 
of the core (Fig. 17), which is rarely the case in 
the Alaskan assemblages discussed in this article. 
Also, even if microblades from the Sumnagin complex are also used unretouched, quite a few are 
also retouched or turned into tools (e.g., sidescrapers, burins, truncated microblades, gravers, borers, 
points) (e.g., Dyakonov 2007; Gómez Coutouly 
2011b), which is not the case in northwestern 
Alaska. I have already argued against such a connection when comparing the Anangula assemblage 
(Aleutian Islands) with the Sumnagin complex 
(Gómez Coutouly 2015).
